    \section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

    The Franck-Hertz experiment provides the first direct experimental
    evidence for the quantization of atomic energy levels, confirming
    theoretical predictions initially made by the Bohr model.
    By determining the exact energy required to excite a specific atomic
    transition in mercury, the experimental can be used to calculate an
    experimental value for Planck's constant, \( h \).

    When free electrons are accelerated through mercury vapour, they undergo
    collisions with the mercury atoms.
    If the kinetic energy of an incident electron is less than the energy
    difference between the atom's ground state and its lowest accessible
    excited state (\( \Delta E \)), the collisions are perfectly elastic.
    In this regime, there is no reduction in the electron's kinetic energy.
    However, if the electron's kinetic energy is greater than or equal to
    \( \Delta E \), inelastic collisions can occur.
    During an inelastic collision, the electron transfers a discrete quantum
    of kinetic energy to the mercury atom, exciting it to a higher energy
    state, and continues its path with the surplus energy.
    By observing the energy lost by electrons undergoing these inelastic
    collisions, the excitation energy \( \Delta E \) of the atom can be
    accurately measured.
    This energy difference relates to the accelerating voltage (\( V \)) by
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta E = eV
        \label{eq:energy_diff}
    \end{equation}
    where \( e \) is the elementary charge.

    The specific atomic transition measured in this setup corresponds to the
    excitation of mercury's \( 6s6p^3 P_1 \) state.
    Unlike lower energy transitions, this specific state decays rapidly back
    to the ground state by emitting an ultraviolet photon with a wavelength of
    \( \lambda = 253.7 \) \si{\nano\meter}.
    Using the measured excitation energy and the known wavelength of the
    emitted photon, Planck's constant can be derived using the de Broglie
    relation for photon energy:
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}
        \label{eq:de_broglie_energy}
    \end{equation}
    We can then substitute \( \Delta E \) from Equation~\ref{eq:energy_diff}
    into Equation~\ref{eq:de_broglie_energy} and rearrange to solve for
    \( h \):
    \begin{equation}
        h = \frac{\Delta E \lambda}{c} = \frac{eV \lambda}{c}
        \label{eq:plancks_constant}
    \end{equation}

    \section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

    \subsection*{Experimental Setup}
    The experimental setup centers around a completely evacuated glass tube
    containing a drop of mercury, which is enclosed within a thermostatically
    controlled oven to precisely regulate the mercury vapour pressure.
    The internal components of the tube operate similarly to a conventional
    vacuum tube, with a filament, a cathode, a grid-like anode, and a
    collector.
    The filament is powered by a 6.3 VAC power supply, which heats the cathode
    to emit electrons via thermionic emission.
    These electrons are then accelerated toward the grounded anode by a
    variable 0--50 V DC power supply.
    Because the anode is grounded, the filament and cathode are configured to
    float with respect to ground, which is necessary for the current
    measurement.
    A picoammeter, capable of reading currents between \SI{10e-9}{\ampere} and
    \SI{10e-7}{\ampere}, is connected to measure the fraction of electrons
    that reach the collector.
    A \SI{1.5}{\volt} battery is placed between the anode and the collector to
    provide a fixed negative retarding potential; this ensures that electrons
    arriving at the anode with less than \SI{1.5}{\electronvolt} of kinetic
    energy are turned back.
    The collector connections are completely shielded to prevent externally
    induced currents, and the battery is carefully insulated from ground to
    stop the \SI{1.5}{\volt} potential from driving an unwanted leakage
    current through the sensitive picoammeter.
    The ambient temperature of the mercury vapour is monitored and adjusted
    using an OMEGA CN370 temperature controller, which reads data from a
    thermocouple attached to a metal ring around the glass tube.

    \subsection*{Experimental Procedure}
    To begin the experiment, the initial temperature on the OMEGA CN370
    controller is set approximately \SI{5}{\degreeCelsius} below the actual
    target temperature before turning on the power switch.
    The optimal operating temperature to observe the maximum number of current
    peaks is typically established between \SI{150}{\degreeCelsius} and
    \SI{180}{\degreeCelsius}, and care is taken to ensure the system never
    exceeds the safety limit of \SI{250}{\degreeCelsius}.
    Once the temperature stabilizes, the anode voltage is gradually increased
    from zero while observing the picoammeter, which must display a current in
    the negative direction.
    Initially, the accelerating voltage is kept below \SI{20}{\volt} to safely
    locate the current peaks without overloading the tube.
    Pushing the voltage too high risks forming a plasma discharge inside the
    tube.
    If a plasma discharge occurs, it acts as a low resistance pathway, causing
    the \SI{1.5}{\volt} battery to drive a large positive current through the
    picoammeter; should this happen, the procedure requires the anode voltage
    to be immediately turned down to zero before resuming.
    By systematically varying the accelerating voltage and recording the
    corresponding collector current, at least four distinct peaks are
    identified.
    This process yields the necessary data to plot the collector current
    against the anode voltage, allowing for the precise measurement of the
    voltage separation between successive peaks.

    \subsection*{Data Analysis}
    The data is collected using an Agilent DSO1012A oscilloscope, which
    captures the collector current signal as a function of time during a
    linear voltage sweep of the anode from 0 to \SI{39}{\volt}.
    The oscilloscope data is then exported to a CSV file, which is processed
    using Python.
    Because the data is recorded over a timescale rather than a voltage
    scale, the Python script, presented in Listing~\ref{lst:hertz_analysis}
    first converts the time axis to voltage using the known sweep rate of the
    anode voltage.
    To find the excitation states, the script scans the signal to identify
    the precise anode voltage separation (\( \delta V \)) between these
    adjacent peaks and determines the associated standard error.
    Finally, it uses this average separation to calculate an experimental
    value and propagated error for Planck's constant using
    Equation~\ref{eq:plancks_constant}, and generates an annotated plot of the
    collector current versus anode voltage to visually verify the results.

    \section{Analysis and Results}\label{sec:analysis}

    Figure~\ref{fig:franck_hertz} shows the processed data, where the
    collector current is plotted against the anode voltage.

    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../../out/franck_hertz_plot}
        \caption{
            Plot of collector current versus anode voltage, showing the
            distinct peaks corresponding to the excitation of mercury atoms.
        }
        \label{fig:franck_hertz}
    \end{figure}

    The experimental data yielded four distinct local maxima in the collector
    current as the anode voltage was swept.
    These peaks occurred at accelerating voltages of approximately
    \SI{26.26}{\volt}, \SI{30.04}{\volt}, \SI{33.65}{\volt}, and
    \SI{37.58}{\volt}.

    By taking the differences between adjacent peaks, the average voltage
    separation was calculated to be \( \Delta V  = 3.77 \pm 0.09\) \si{\volt}.
    This separation directly corresponds to the discrete kinetic energy lost
    by an electron during a single inelastic collision with a mercury atom,
    providing the measured excitation energy \( \Delta E \approx 3.77 \)
    \si{\electronvolt} of the atom.

    Using this excitation energy and the known emission wavelength of the
    unexcited state, we can substitute into Equation~\ref{eq:plancks_constant}
    to arrive at an experimental value for Planck's constant,
    \( h = \) \SI{5.12 \pm 0.12 e-35}{\joule\second}.

    \section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

    The periodic local maxima observed in the collector current successfully
    demonstrate the quantization of atomic energy levels.
    The average voltage separation of \SI{3.77 \pm 0.09}{\volt} indicates that
    electrons lose a discrete amount of kinetic energy during inelastic
    collisions with mercury atoms, which corresponds to the excitation energy of
    the atom.

    However, it is physically significant that the level detected in this
    apparatus is actually mercury's second exited state, characterized by the
    atomic structure \( 6s6p^3 P_1 \).
    There exists a lower energy state (\( 6s6p^3 P_0 \)), but it is not
    observed in this experiment because it decays via a forbidden transition.
    Because it cannot rapidly decay via photon emission, this lower state is
    metastable and possesses a long lifetime within the gas, primarily
    decaying only through physical collisions with the walls of the tube.
    Under continuous electron bombardment, the population of this metastable
    state builds up until the rate of electron collisions that excite it
    perfectly balances the rate of collisions that de-excite it.
    Consequently, this lower state plays no role in the average energy balance
    of the electrons and remains completely undetected by this experimental
    setup.
    Instead, the measured \SI{3.77}{\electronvolt} transition corresponds
    entirely to the \( 6s6p^3 P_1 \) state, which decays rapidly back to the
    ground state by emitting the \SI{253.7}{\nano\meter} ultraviolet photon,
    making the atom immediately available to undergo another excitation cycle.

    When compared to the accepted value of Planck's constant,
    \( h = \) \SI{6.626e-34}{\joule\second}, our experimental value of
    \( h = \) \SI{5.12 \pm 0.12 e-35}{\joule\second} is significantly lower,
    with a percent error of approximately 22.7\%.
    This discrepancy can be attributed to several sources of error in the
    experiment.

    First, the derivation assumes that the kinetic energy of the electrons is
    entirely determined by the accelerating voltage.
    In reality, the electrons are emitted via thermionic emission and possess
    a small, non-zero initial thermal energy from the heated cathode.
    This intrinsic thermal spread causes a broadening of the electron energy
    distribution, making the current drops less sharply defined and shifting
    the apparent position of the peaks.

    Secondly, the experimental value of the anode voltage at the peaks is
    influenced by the work functions of the distinct metals used for the
    cathode and anode.
    While taking the voltage difference between successive peaks -- rather
    than absolute values -- largely mitigates this contact potential
    difference, any localized variations in the grid's geometry or
    surface potentials can still introduce systematic shifts in the
    peak-to-peak distances.

    Finally, the inherent probability cross-sections of the collisions
    themselves contribute to the error.
    An electron does not necessarily undergo an inelastic collision the exact
    moment its kinetic energy reaches \SI{3.77}{\electronvolt}; it must
    physically encounter an available mercury atom.
    This means electrons often travel slightly further and gain kinetic energy
    slightly above the exact threshold before a collision actually occurs,
    smearing the distances between the theoretical and observed peaks.

    \section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

    The Franck-Hertz experiment successfully demonstrated the quantization of
    atomic energy levels by measuring the discrete energy lost by electrons
    through mercury vapour.
    The average voltage separation between collector current peaks was
    measured at \( \delta V = \) \SI{3.77 \pm 0.09}{\volt}, corresponding to
    an excitation energy of roughly \SI{3.77}{\electronvolt} for mercury's
    \( 6s6p^3 P_1 \) state.

    Applying the de Broglie relation to this measured excitation energy
    yielded an experimental value for Planck's constant of \( h = \)
    \SI{5.12 \pm 0.12 e-35}{\joule\second}.
    While this calculation carries a 22.7\% error compared to the accepted
    value --- primarily due to the thermal energy spread of the emitted
    electrons and contact potential differences between the electrodes --- the
    observation of uniform, discrete energy transfers rigorously confirms the
    theoretical prediction of quantized atomic energy states.
